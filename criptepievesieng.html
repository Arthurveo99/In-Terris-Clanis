Città della Pieve (PG)

Le Cripte di Città della Pieve

A Buried World in the Heart of the Ancient City

Beneath the surface of Città della Pieve lies a dimension suspended in time. Its crypts, tunnels, cellars, and underground chambers testify to a long history of religious, civil, and defensive stratification. Among these ancient spaces, the crypt of the Co-Cathedral of Saints Gervasius and Protasius stands out for its historical and architectural significance, still preserving the austere and mysterious atmosphere of the medieval origins of the church.

Origins and Structure of the Cathedral Crypt

The crypt is located beneath the presbytery of the current cathedral, which was rebuilt starting in the 13th century and repeatedly renovated until the 18th century. However, the foundations of the crypt most likely date back to the Romanesque period, when an earlier sacred building dedicated to the martyr saints Gervasius and Protasius, co-patrons of the city, stood on this site.

The space appears as a small rectangular hall with groin vaults resting on central stone columns, some with simple capitals, possibly reused from earlier buildings. The bare walls, partially covered with ancient plaster, evoke the austerity of early medieval liturgy.

In the dim light, one can still make out traces of devotional frescoes, now heavily deteriorated, depicting saints and ornamental motifs from the 14th century. It is likely that the crypt originally housed a small altar or a reliquary niche for the veneration of relics, as was customary at the time.

Liturgical Function and the Cult of Saints

In the Middle Ages, crypts were considered the holiest part of a church, a sort of “sanctuary within the sanctuary.” They were used to preserve relics, celebrate votive Masses, and bury important ecclesiastical figures. According to local sources, the crypt of the Duomo of Città della Pieve may have held the relics of Saints Gervasius and Protasius, whose cult was widespread in northern and central Italy.

In later centuries, the crypt was also used as a burial site for canons and for some noble families tied to the church, following an ancient custom reserved for those with special religious or social privileges. Some worn tomb slabs, visible in the floor, seem to confirm this use.

Accessibility and Conservation

The crypt is not normally open to the public but is occasionally accessible during cultural events or solemn religious celebrations. Its conservation is a concern for the diocese and local cultural organizations, who recognize its high historical and spiritual value.

Recent inspections and archaeological surveys have confirmed the stability of the structure, though with some issues related to humidity and ventilation. Studies are underway for a preservation project that may allow safe public access, potentially through guided tours and underground routes connected to other hypogean spaces in the city.

Other Underground Spaces: Tunnels, Cellars, and Legends

Città della Pieve, built largely on a ridge of compact tuff, is rich in underground cavities carved over the centuries. Many houses in the historic center—especially along Via Pietro Vannucci and Via della Corgna—preserve medieval or Renaissance cellars, often interconnected and equipped with small staircases, niches, and wells. Some of these spaces were used as shelters during wars and invasions.

Local legends are not lacking: it is said that a secret tunnel connects the Duomo to the Rocca Perugina, a fortress built in the 14th century by order of the city of Perugia. This underground passage was allegedly used, according to oral tradition, as an escape route or a hidden connection between religious and military power. However, no concrete archaeological evidence has yet been found to support this claim.

A Heritage to Rediscover

The crypts and underground spaces of Città della Pieve represent a hidden heritage, little known even to local residents, yet full of meaning and cultural potential. They form a deep connection with the city's millennia-old history—its faiths, fears, and hopes.

To enhance and protect them means to bring to light the city's deepest memory and to create a new way of sharing it with visitors and future generations.
