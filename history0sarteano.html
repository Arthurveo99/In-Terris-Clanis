History of Sarteano: from the Neolithic to the Roman Era

1. The Neolithic and the First Human Presence (6000–3000 BC)

The oldest archaeological evidence from Sarteano dates back to the Neolithic, a period when humans transitioned from a nomadic to a settled lifestyle, practicing agriculture, animal husbandry, and pottery. In the hilly areas surrounding the town—especially around Mount Cetona and the Sarteano highlands—tools made of flint and obsidian, arrowheads, scrapers, and pottery shards have been found.

The area was inhabited or frequented by small sedentary communities who lived in huts and settled near water sources and natural resources, such as the caves of Mount Cetona. Some of these caves were also used as collective burial sites.

2. The Copper and Bronze Ages (3000–1000 BC)

During the Copper Age (or Chalcolithic), human groups began using copper alongside stone to craft tools. Archaeological discoveries around Sarteano (especially near Mount Cetona and the Val d'Orcia) show the presence of small cave tombs, decorated pottery, and metal artifacts.

In the Bronze Age, around the 2nd millennium BC, more stable settlements emerged on strategic high grounds—such as the hill where the Castle of Sarteano now stands. It is believed that this elevation was already a fortified or sacred site, given its dominant position. This period also saw the development of complex burial rituals, including both inhumation and cremation tombs.

3. The Iron Age and the Rise of the Etruscans (9th–6th century BC)

Sarteano first became part of the Protovillanovan world, and later fully integrated into the Etruscan civilization. This marks the most well-documented and celebrated period of the town’s ancient history.

The Sarteano area is renowned for the Pianacce necropolis, one of the most significant Etruscan funerary complexes of inland Tuscany, dating back to the 6th–5th century BC. The tombs, carved into the rock—often with rectangular layouts or chamber plans—reveal the presence of a structured, hierarchical society.

The Tomb of the Infernal Chariot, discovered in 2003, stands as one of the most extraordinary examples of early Etruscan art. Its wall paintings depict a chariot drawn by lions and panthers, driven by a demon with a fiery red face—an image unique in the Etruscan world, possibly reflecting beliefs about the afterlife influenced by Eastern or Italic traditions.

Beyond the necropolises, it is believed that an Etruscan settlement existed on or near the current castle site, though it remains partly unexplored. The Etruscans of Sarteano were craftsmen, farmers, and merchants, closely connected to nearby city-states such as Chiusi (Clevsi), with which Sarteano maintained strong ties.

4. The Roman Period (3rd century BC – 5th century AD)

With the gradual Roman conquest of Tuscany (3rd century BC), Sarteano too came under the influence of Rome. This was not a brutal Romanization, but rather a process of cultural and administrative assimilation, which integrated the Etruscan population into Roman systems.

The territory of Sarteano was crossed by secondary roads that connected the Via Cassia to the settlements of the Val di Chiana. The Romans made strategic use of hill routes for both military and commercial purposes.

During the Imperial period, the landscape was transformed by agrarian centuriation: parcels of land were assigned to veterans or Roman families. Remains of rural villas and agricultural settlements have been discovered in the surrounding countryside, where viticulture, grain cultivation, and livestock farming were practiced.

These Roman villas were often equipped with bath complexes and other elaborate structures, and some rural buildings were likely reused in the following centuries.

With the crisis of the 3rd to 5th centuries AD, many villas were abandoned or turned into small fortified villages. The presence of early Christian worship sites and Late Antiquity burials in the area indicates the rise of rural Christianity, which would lay the foundation for the early medieval period.
